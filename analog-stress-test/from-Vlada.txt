Just some general technical background inputs from my side – related to the attached document.

What we see from the attached slides, the gate oxide and the drain drift region have completely different physical limits.
Even though they belong to the same transistor, the oxide and the drift region break for totally different reasons.

1. VGS Stress (gate oxide screening) – targets gate oxide integrity
- VGS stress = 1.2–1.4× nominal gate rating
- Typical values: 6–8 V for HV MOS, 4–5 V for IO, 2.2–3 V for thin-oxide
- Gate oxide is a vertical dielectric — extremely field‑sensitive.

Failure modes detected:
- Process oxide defects
- Plasma charging damage
- Oxide thinning, pinholes
- Gate leakage
- Hot-carrier injection near gate edge

2. VDS Stress – targets drain junction robustness, drift region quality and avalanche-related weaknesses.

In a modern BCD LDMOS or DMOS, the drain-to-source voltage (VDS) is tolerated because of:

a) Extended drift region
A long, lightly doped region spreads the electric field laterally away from the channel.

b) Buried layer (BL) and deep‑N well
These provide:
- vertical electric field spreading
- improved punch‑through immunity
- higher breakdown margin

c) Deep trench isolation (DTI)
DTI separates device edges – prevents premature surface breakdown.

d) Field plates
Overlapping metal layers shape electric fields to reduce peaks near the drain.

e) LOCOS/STI field oxides
Allow larger depletion regions. All these features together support:
- 20 V
- 40 V
- 60 V
- 80 V
- sometimes 100–120 V BVdss (B – comes from Break) ratings

These regions can safely take very high VDS stress even while the gate oxide cannot.
Thus, in the PDK:
- VDS screen may be 1.3–1.6× BVdss (e.g., 40V device stressed at 55–60V)

Failure modes detected:
- Incorrect drift doping
- Field plate misalignment
- Buried‑layer defects
- STI/DTI leakage
- Microplasma breakdown
- Punch‑through issues
